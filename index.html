Tokyo Trip Overview Per Person

Assumptions used for planning: 14 to 21 nights in Tokyo, mid-range spending, Tokyo accommodation booked separately, and day plans chosen flexibly by weather and energy level rather than fixed dates.

What I verified first

  • Christmas is not a Japanese public holiday, so the idea that Tokyo shops universally close for two days over Christmas is not generally true. Many shops, restaurants, illuminations, and commercial areas stay open.
  • The bigger closure window is New Year, especially January 1 and sometimes January 2 to January 3 for smaller shops, museums, and independent businesses.
  • For a Tokyo-heavy trip, a long nationwide rail pass is usually poor value. A mix of IC card travel plus a few targeted regional passes is more sensible.

Important pass assumption

  • Without pass means normal pay-as-you-go transit with an IC card or regular tickets.
  • With pass below means you are using the Tokyo Subway Ticket efficiently on the right kind of city day.
  • There is not a normal 2-week Tokyo tourist transit pass that cleanly fits this trip, so I am not assuming a fictional all-trip Tokyo pass.
  • The realistic tourist pass option for Tokyo city days is the Tokyo Subway Ticket, currently:
  • 24 hours: JPY 1,000 per person
  • 48 hours: JPY 1,500 per person
  • 72 hours: JPY 2,000 per person
  • It only covers Tokyo Metro + Toei Subway, so it does not replace JR, private railways, or most longer day-trip transport.
  • The pass is most useful when you deliberately group 2 to 3 subway-heavy Tokyo days into one 48-hour or 72-hour window.

1-night hot-spring town options from Tokyo

1. Hakone

  • Why it works: easiest classic onsen option from Tokyo, strong ryokan selection, art museums, mountain scenery, many private-bath options.
  • Access: about 85 to 120 minutes depending on route and lodging area.
  • Good pass: Hakone Freepass
  • Official area guide: Hakone Navi
  • Strong ryokan examples:
  • Hakone Kowakien Ten-yu
  • Mount View Hakone
  • Price reality: usually roughly JPY 17,500 to 45,000+ per night per person with dinner/breakfast depending on room and bath type.
  • Best fit: strongest all-round option.

2. Kawaguchiko

  • Why it works: best chance of dramatic winter Fuji views plus lakeside ryokan stays.
  • Access: about 2 to 2.5 hours.
  • Official area guide: Fujikawaguchiko Tourism
  • Strong ryokan examples:
  • Konansou
  • Kozantei Ubuya
  • Price reality: often JPY 20,000 to 50,000+ per night per person with better Fuji-view rooms costing more.
  • Best fit: most romantic/scenic if weather is clear.

3. Atami

  • Why it works: easy rail access, seaside setting, easier for a simple 1-night break than a complex mountain transfer day.
  • Access: roughly 40 to 90 minutes depending on train.
  • Official area guide: Atami City Tourism
  • Ryokan examples:
  • Sakuraya Ryokan
  • Furuya Ryokan
  • Price reality: often JPY 15,000 to 40,000+ per night per person.
  • Best fit: easiest low-friction overnight if you want less transit.

4. Kinugawa Onsen

  • Why it works: classic onsen town and pairs well with Nikko if you want temples one day and a ryokan stay the next.
  • Access: about 2 to 2.5 hours.
  • Official area guide: Nikko City Tourism / Kinugawa
  • Ryokan examples:
  • Asaya Hotel
  • Kinugawa Kanaya Hotel
  • Price reality: roughly JPY 17,500 to 45,000+ per night per person depending on room and meal plan.
  • Best fit: best if you already know you want Nikko-region scenery.

Other worthwhile 1-night trips besides onsen towns

These are the strongest overnight ideas I found that are meaningfully different from a classic onsen stay. My honest take: some are clearly worth a night, while others are more situational and only make sense if they match your style very well.

1. Central Nikko or Okunikko without making the trip “about onsen”

  • Why it works: this is one of the best cases for a non-onsen overnight because a single day in Nikko can feel rushed, especially in winter with shorter daylight.
  • Best for:
  • World Heritage shrines and temples at a slower pace
  • adding Kanmangafuchi, Tamozawa Villa, or a second nature stop
  • seeing Nikko in early morning or evening when the day-trip crowds thin out
  • Access: about 2 hours from Tokyo depending on train choice
  • Official trip-planning base:
  • Central Nikko
  • Getting Here & Around
  • Rough lodging reality per person:
  • simple hotel / guesthouse: about JPY 6,000 to 12,000
  • nicer heritage-style or scenic hotel: about JPY 12,500 to 22,500+
  • Best fit: one of the strongest non-onsen overnights in the whole set

2. Mount Mitake overnight in a shukubo or mountain inn

  • Why it works: this is one of the most different-feeling easy escapes from Tokyo, and it stays within Tokyo prefecture while still feeling like a real retreat.
  • Best for:
  • mountain atmosphere
  • shrine culture
  • a slower, quieter, more traditional overnight
  • doing the mountain in the late afternoon and early morning rather than as a rushed day trip
  • Access: roughly 2 hours including train, bus, cable car, and a final uphill walk
  • Official planning base:
  • Ome City sightseeing guide
  • Mitake hiking course
  • example shukubo: Mitake Sanso
  • Rough lodging reality per person:
  • shukubo / simple mountain inn: about JPY 9,000 to 16,000
  • Best fit: strongest pick if you want something spiritual, wintry, and genuinely different

3. Shimoda or the Izu coast as a seaside overnight

  • Why it works: if you want “coast and atmosphere” rather than “onsen and ryokan,” this is the most convincing nearby alternative.
  • Best for:
  • a winter sea-coast mood
  • black-ship history / opening-of-Japan history
  • geopark scenery and dramatic coastal formations
  • a calmer break in the middle of a Tokyo-heavy trip
  • Access: usually around 2.5 to 3.5 hours depending on route and exact town
  • Official planning base:
  • Shimoda City on B-Izu
  • Official Izu Travel Guide B-Izu
  • Izu Peninsula Geopark
  • Rough lodging reality per person:
  • basic hotel / guesthouse: about JPY 7,000 to 13,000
  • nicer seaside stay: about JPY 12,500 to 25,000+
  • Best fit: best non-onsen overnight if you want the sea more than shrines or mountain hiking

4. Narita temple-town overnight

  • Why it works: this is less “special destination overnight” and more “easy atmospheric one-night break” with strong practical value.
  • Best for:
  • Edo-style temple-town walking
  • a very low-stress overnight before or after a long flight
  • a quieter historic stop that most Tokyo visitors skip
  • Access: about 1 to 1.5 hours from Tokyo
  • Official planning base:
  • Narita easy-to-understand guide
  • Top things to see and do
  • Make the most of your stay in Narita
  • Rough lodging reality per person:
  • straightforward city/airport hotel: about JPY 5,000 to 12,000
  • nicer traditional-leaning or larger hotel: about JPY 10,000 to 17,500
  • Best fit: worthwhile, but more for convenience and mood than for “must-do overnight”

Best non-onsen 1-night picks overall

  1. Nikko overnight if you want the highest sightseeing payoff
  2. Mount Mitake overnight if you want the most unusual / atmospheric short escape
  3. Shimoda or the Izu coast if you want a winter seaside break
  4. Narita if you want the easiest low-effort overnight with temple-town character

My recommendation

  • If you only add one non-onsen overnight: choose Nikko overnight or Mount Mitake overnight.
  • If you want something romantic and scenic: choose Shimoda / Izu coast.
  • If you want the easiest add-on with minimal logistics: choose Narita.
  • If you already do an onsen overnight, I would probably not add another separate 1-night side trip unless the total trip is closer to 3 weeks than 2 weeks.

Food and drinks to actively try

This list is meant to help with actual eating choices during the trip, not just famous names. It mixes restaurant dishes with easy store, depachika, and convenience-store finds.

Strong winter and seasonal picks

  • Seasonal oden: one of the easiest winter comfort foods in Tokyo, available in izakaya and often at convenience stores in colder months. GO TOKYO specifically highlights it as a classic winter Tokyo food.
  • Seasonal nabe hot pots: especially chanko nabe, sukiyaki, shabu-shabu, and motsu nabe. Best on very cold evenings or after a long outdoor day.
  • Seasonal yakiimo: roasted sweet potato sold from specialty shops, supermarkets, and sometimes convenience stores. Very good as a walking snack on cold days.
  • Seasonal mikan and winter strawberries: easy supermarket or depachika buy, good for hotel breakfasts or train snacks.
  • Seasonal amazake: a sweet, warming rice drink often found around shrine/temple areas and winter events.
  • Seasonal winter sake styles: especially namazake, nigori, and warm atsukan. JNTO notes that winter is a key sake season and that namazake appears in winter.

Store and depachika things worth trying

  • Good onigiri from convenience stores, station food halls, or depachika counters. Try salmon, ume, tuna mayo, or more regional fillings if you see them.
  • Tamago sando from convenience stores or bakeries.
  • Karaage from depachika prepared-food counters.
  • Tamagoyaki from department-store food halls or market areas.
  • Mochi and seasonal wagashi from old shops in Yanaka, Kagurazaka, Asakusa-adjacent areas, or department-store sweets floors.
  • Japanese pudding, milk desserts, and limited seasonal sweets from convenience stores if you want low-effort snack research.
  • Bottled tea comparison: try at least one unsweetened green tea, hojicha, and milk tea from vending machines or convenience stores.

Restaurant dishes and food experiences worth prioritizing

  • Sushi breakfast or early lunch at Toyosu if you are already doing the Toyosu morning.
  • Tempura omakase or high-quality tempura lunch at least once.
  • Yakitori with beer or highball in a small neighborhood spot rather than only in a tourist-heavy chain.
  • Tonkatsu at a specialist shop.
  • Soba, especially on colder days when you want something excellent but not heavy.
  • Ramen in a style you actually like rather than “the most famous queue.” Shoyu, shio, and lighter fish-based bowls are often a better fit if you are eating several rich meals on the trip.
  • Monjayaki if you want one very Tokyo-specific casual meal.
  • Kissaten toast, pudding, or omurice for a retro cafe meal rather than only third-wave coffee.

Drinks to try

  • Sake flight at an izakaya or specialty bar.
  • Japanese whisky highball.
  • Hoppy if you want an older-school Tokyo bar drink.
  • Craft beer in Ebisu, Kiyosumi-Shirakawa, or Nakameguro.
  • Hojicha latte or matcha latte as cold-weather cafe breaks.

Best moments to use this list

  • Very cold day: oden, nabe, hot sake, amazake
  • Clear walking day: yakiimo, coffee, depachika picnic food
  • After nightlife: ramen, onigiri, pudding, Pocari Sweat
  • Train day-trip morning: onigiri, egg sandwich, bottled tea, fruit from a station or department store food hall
